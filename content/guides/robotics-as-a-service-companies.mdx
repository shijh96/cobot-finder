## Why Robotics as a Service Is Transforming Manufacturing

The upfront cost of buying and deploying a cobot system has always been a barrier for small and mid-size manufacturers. A complete deployment — including the arm, gripper, integration, and training — can cost $50,000 to $150,000. For a job shop or seasonal operation, that investment is hard to justify.

Enter **[Robotics as a Service (RaaS)](/guides/robotics-as-a-service)**: a subscription-based model where companies pay a monthly or hourly fee to use robots for specific tasks, with the service provider handling ownership, maintenance, and support. The RaaS market is growing rapidly. Market research firm Grand View Research projects the global RaaS market to reach $23.3 billion by 2030, up from $4.2 billion in 2025 — a **41% compound annual growth rate**.

RaaS is particularly attractive for [manufacturers](/guides/cobots-in-manufacturing) facing labor shortages, seasonal demand swings, or the need to validate automation before committing capital. Instead of asking "Can we afford to buy a robot?" you ask "Can we afford **not** to automate this process?"

This guide profiles 10 of the leading RaaS companies, compares their pricing models and specialties, and provides criteria to help you choose the right provider for your operation.

## The 10 Best Robotics as a Service Companies

### 1. [Formic](https://formic.co/) — Hourly-Priced Cobot Welding

**Overview**
Formic is a Chicago-based RaaS startup that brings cobot welding to small and mid-size job shops and contract manufacturers. Instead of requiring shops to invest $80,000-$150,000 in a complete welding cell, Formic handles ownership and deploys cobots on a simple hourly pricing model.

**Pricing**
- $8-$24 per hour (depending on weld complexity, location, and ancillary services)
- No setup fees or long-term contracts
- Billing tied directly to robot runtime

**Industries & Applications**
- Metal fabrication job shops
- Contract welding
- High-mix, low-volume production
- Off-season/seasonal welding overflow

**Key Features**
- **Neon AI welding control:** Proprietary weld quality monitoring to reduce defects
- End-to-end model: Formic handles equipment, maintenance, and programming
- Transparent pricing — you only pay for hours the robot actually works
- Can handle both MIG and TIG welding

**Best For**
Small job shops and fabricators that want to automate welding **without capital expenditure**. Ideal for shops with seasonal demand or those testing whether cobot welding fits their process.

**Pros**
- Lowest barrier to entry in cobot welding
- Formic owns maintenance liability
- Pay-as-you-go removes financial risk

**Cons**
- Hourly rates add up quickly (40 hrs/week = $1,600-$4,800/month)
- Less cost-effective for continuous, high-volume welding (you may be better off buying)
- Limited customization for specialized weld types

They operate a RaaS model aimed at enterprise customers wanting unified control across multiple robot types and locations.

**Pricing**
- Custom per deployment (typically $5k-$15k/month for enterprise contracts)
- Pricing depends on robot type, number of robots, and ForgeOS features used
- 12-24 month minimum contracts

**Industries & Applications**
- Large manufacturers with existing robot fleets
- Multi-site operations
- Complex automation workflows requiring cross-robot coordination

**Key Features**
- **ForgeOS platform:** Unified programming, monitoring, and analytics across all robot brands
- One programming interface for Universal Robots, Fanuc, KUKA, ABB, Doosan, etc.
- Advanced analytics: uptime tracking, cycle time optimization, utilization dashboards
- Mobile app for remote monitoring and troubleshooting
- Workflow automation (trigger rules, sequencing, error handling)

**Best For**
Large manufacturers and integrators managing 10+ robots across multiple sites and brands. Not for startups or small shops.

**Pros**
- Eliminates vendor lock-in (supports all major brands)
- Software-first approach reduces robot dependency
- Powerful analytics accelerate optimization

**Cons**
- High minimum contract values ($5k+/month)
- Steep learning curve for teams new to software-centric automation
- Designed for enterprise scale, not DIY shops

**Industries & Applications**

- Automotive subassembly
- Plants with existing robot infrastructure needing safety upgrades

**Key Features**
- **SafetyOS:** AI vision continuously monitors human presence and distance
- Works with any industrial robot (not limited to cobots)
- Replaces expensive safety fencing and barriers
- Enables traditional robots to safely work in collaborative spaces
- Real-time alerts and audit logs

**Best For**
Manufacturers with older, traditional industrial robots who want to enable human-robot collaboration **without replacing the robot**. Also ideal for space-constrained facilities where physical fencing is impractical.

**Pros**
- Turns existing robots into quasi-collaborative robots
- Eliminates cost and complexity of physical safety barriers
- Advanced AI for edge-case detection

**Cons**

## How to Choose the Right RaaS Provider

Selecting a RaaS solution requires evaluating your specific needs against each provider's strengths:

### 1. Application Type

**Welding?** → Formic or Hirebotics
**Machine tending?** → Rapid Robotics
**Warehouse picking/transport?** → Locus Robotics, 6 River Systems, Fetch Robotics
**Variable-item picking?** → Plus One Robotics, Covariant
**Safety monitoring (existing robots)?** → Veo Robotics
**Multi-robot orchestration (large scale)?** → Ready Robotics

### 2. Capital vs. Operational Expense

If you **cannot justify capex**, RaaS shifts the burden to monthly opex. But calculate the **3-year total cost**:

- Buying a cobot system: $80,000 (capex) + $15,000 (maintenance/3 years) = $95,000
- RaaS for 3 years: $2,500/month × 36 months = $90,000

Use our [cobot ROI calculator](/guides/cobot-roi-calculator) to model your specific scenario. For lower-utilization equipment or seasonal demand, RaaS typically wins. For high-utilization (80%+ uptime), buying may be cheaper after year 2-3.

### 3. Utilization & Contract Length

High-utilization, continuous operations? → Buy (RaaS accumulates cost)
Seasonal or variable demand? → RaaS (avoid idle capex)
Testing/validation? → RaaS (minimize risk)
Month-to-month flexibility needed? → Hirebotics (most flexible)
Okay with 12-24 month commitment? → Locus, Fetch, 6 River (better pricing)

### 4. Integration Complexity

Simple plug-and-play? → Rapid Robotics, Hirebotics, Formic
Requires WMS integration? → Locus Robotics, Fetch Robotics
Existing robot fleet control needed? → Ready Robotics
Standalone computer vision system? → Plus One Robotics, Covariant

### 5. Vendor Lock-In Risk

Most RaaS providers use standard robots ([Universal Robots, Fanuc](/guides/best-cobot-arms)) you could buy if you leave. However:
- **Formic, Hirebotics, Rapid Robotics** use proprietary software (medium lock-in)
- **Locus, 6 River, Fetch** use proprietary fleet software (high lock-in)
- **Ready Robotics** explicitly supports multiple brands (low lock-in)

## When RaaS Makes Sense (And When It Doesn't)

### RaaS is a Good Fit For:

- **Testing automation before committing capital** — Try for 3-6 months, then decide to buy
- **Seasonal or variable demand** — Robot runs April-September, sits idle the rest
- **Small operations with limited capital** — $50,000+ capex investment is out of reach
- **Highly specialized or changing applications** — Task mix changes quarterly; RaaS allows flexibility
- **Rapid scaling** — Go from 1 robot to 10 robots without major capex outlays
- **Validating labor cost savings** — See real ROI numbers before locking into ownership

### RaaS is NOT a Good Fit For:

- **24/7 high-utilization operations** — After 2-3 years, buying is more cost-effective
- **Custom, proprietary equipment integration** — RaaS providers cannot handle one-off integrations
- **Highly confidential processes** — Allowing a third party to operate equipment in your facility
- **Strict long-term cost control** — RaaS introduces ongoing subscription costs (less predictable than ownership)
- **Remote/geographically isolated facilities** — Support and maintenance logistics become expensive

## The RaaS Future: Key Trends

**1. AI-Powered Optimization**
Providers like Locus and Covariant are embedding machine learning into their platforms. Each deployment makes the next deployment better. Expect rapid improvements in picking speed, error rates, and efficiency over the next 2-3 years.

**2. Horizontal Software Platforms**
Ready Robotics' multi-brand approach is winning. Expect more software-first players that abstract away robot hardware, letting customers mix-and-match equipment.

**3. Downward Price Pressure**
As competition increases, monthly subscription costs are trending down. Locus dropped prices 20% in 2024. Expect $2,000-$3,000/month to become the floor for warehouse AMRs.

**4. Integration with E-Commerce Platforms**
6 River's Shopify integration is the template. Expect deep integrations with WMS, TMS, and order management systems. "Click to automate" will become the standard.

**5. Niche-Specific Solutions**
Broad-market RaaS providers (Locus, Fetch) will face competition from laser-focused vendors (Plus One for variable picking, Veo for safety). Specialization beats generalization.
