The upfront cost of buying and deploying a cobot system has always been a barrier for small and mid-size manufacturers. A complete deployment — including the arm, gripper, integration, and training — can cost $50,000 to $150,000. For a job shop or seasonal operation, that investment is hard to justify.

**[Robotics as a Service (RaaS)](/guides/robotics-as-a-service)** flips the model: companies pay a monthly or hourly fee to use robots for specific tasks, with the service provider handling ownership, maintenance, and support. The RaaS market is growing rapidly — Grand View Research projects the global RaaS market to reach **$23.3 billion by 2030**, up from $4.2 billion in 2025, a **41% compound annual growth rate**.

RaaS is particularly attractive for [manufacturers](/guides/cobots-in-manufacturing) facing labor shortages, seasonal demand swings, or the need to validate automation before committing capital. This guide profiles 10 of the leading RaaS companies, compares their pricing models and specialties, and provides criteria to help you choose the right provider.

## The 10 Best Robotics as a Service Companies

### 1. [Formic](https://formic.co/) — Hourly-Priced Cobot Welding

Formic is a Chicago-based RaaS startup that brings cobot welding to small and mid-size job shops and contract manufacturers. Instead of requiring shops to invest $80,000–$150,000 in a complete welding cell, Formic deploys cobots on a simple hourly pricing model.

**Pricing:** $8–$24 per hour (depending on weld complexity). No setup fees or long-term contracts. Billing tied directly to robot runtime.

**Industries:** Metal fabrication job shops, contract welding, high-mix low-volume production, seasonal welding overflow.

**Key Features:**
- **Neon AI welding control** — proprietary weld quality monitoring to reduce defects
- End-to-end model: Formic handles equipment, maintenance, and programming
- Transparent pricing — you only pay for hours the robot actually works
- Supports both MIG and TIG welding

**Best For:** Small job shops and fabricators that want to automate welding **without capital expenditure**. Ideal for shops with seasonal demand or those testing whether cobot welding fits their process.

**Pros:** Lowest barrier to entry in cobot welding. Pay-as-you-go removes financial risk. Formic owns all maintenance liability.

**Cons:** Hourly rates add up quickly (40 hrs/week = $1,600–$4,800/month). Less cost-effective for continuous, high-volume welding. Limited customization for specialized weld types.

### 2. [Rapid Robotics](https://www.rapidrobotics.com/) — Plug-and-Play Machine Tending

Based in San Francisco, Rapid Robotics specializes in pre-engineered "machine tending kits" that automate the loading and unloading of CNC machines, injection molders, and stamping presses. Their robots arrive pre-programmed for common equipment.

**Pricing:** $2,100 per month (all-inclusive). Minimum 3-month contract. Includes hardware, software, maintenance, support, installation, and basic programming.

**Industries:** CNC machine shops, injection molding facilities, stamping operations, job shops with multiple machines.

**Key Features:**
- Pre-engineered kits for Haas, Mazak, DMG Mori, Trumpf, and Husky equipment
- Cobots (Universal Robots) paired with gripper, vision, and safety certification
- Cloud-based monitoring dashboard
- Integrated I/O communication with machine controllers

**Best For:** Mid-size job shops and manufacturers running 2–6 CNC or molding machines. The sweet spot is machine tending on classic industrial equipment.

**Pros:** Truly plug-and-play with minimal integration required. Highly predictable monthly cost. Strong technical support.

**Cons:** $2,100/month may not pencil for low-duty-cycle machines. Pre-engineered kits limit customization. Minimum 3-month commitment.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Best entry point for small shops">
<p>If you are new to RaaS and run a CNC or molding shop, <strong>Rapid Robotics</strong> and <strong>Hirebotics</strong> offer the lowest-risk entry points — predictable monthly pricing, minimal integration, and short minimum contracts.</p>

### 3. [Ready Robotics](https://www.readyrobotics.com/) — Enterprise Platform for Multi-Brand Robots

Columbus, Ohio-based Ready Robotics built **ForgeOS**, a software platform for orchestrating and managing robots from any manufacturer (Universal Robots, Fanuc, [KUKA](https://www.kuka.com/), Doosan, etc.). They operate a RaaS model aimed at enterprise customers wanting unified control across multiple robot types and locations.

**Pricing:** Custom per deployment (typically $5k–$15k/month for enterprise contracts). 12–24 month minimum contracts.

**Industries:** Large manufacturers with existing robot fleets, multi-site operations, complex automation workflows requiring cross-robot coordination.

**Key Features:**
- **ForgeOS platform** — unified programming, monitoring, and analytics across all robot brands
- One programming interface for Universal Robots, Fanuc, KUKA, ABB, Doosan
- Advanced analytics: uptime tracking, cycle time optimization, utilization dashboards
- Mobile app for remote monitoring and troubleshooting

**Best For:** Large manufacturers and integrators managing 10+ robots across multiple sites and brands.

**Pros:** Eliminates vendor lock-in. Software-first approach reduces robot dependency. Powerful analytics.

**Cons:** High minimum contract values. Steep learning curve for teams new to software-centric automation. Not for small shops.

### 4. [Veo Robotics](https://www.veorobotics.com/) — AI-Powered Safety Monitoring

Waltham, Massachusetts-based Veo Robotics focuses on **safe human-robot collaboration monitoring**. Their AI vision system replaces traditional safety fences and light curtains, allowing robots to operate safely around humans using computer vision and real-time decision algorithms.

**Pricing:** Custom (typically $8k–$20k/month per deployment). Setup and integration support included.

**Industries:** Heavy equipment manufacturing, aerospace component assembly, automotive subassembly, plants needing safety upgrades.

**Key Features:**
- **SafetyOS** — AI vision continuously monitors human presence and distance
- Works with any industrial robot (not limited to cobots)
- Replaces expensive safety fencing and barriers
- Enables traditional robots to safely work in collaborative spaces

**Best For:** Manufacturers with older industrial robots who want to enable human-robot collaboration **without replacing the robot**.

**Pros:** Turns existing robots into quasi-collaborative robots. Eliminates cost of physical safety barriers. Advanced AI for edge-case detection.

**Cons:** Subscription cost on top of existing robot ownership. Requires robust camera infrastructure. Not as inherently safe as force-limited cobots.

### 5. [Locus Robotics](https://locusrobotics.com/) — AI-Optimized Warehouse Picking

Wilmington, Massachusetts-based Locus Robotics is the leading provider of **Autonomous Mobile Robots (AMRs) for warehouse pick-to-cart operations**. Their robots navigate warehouse floors, transport bins, and work alongside human pickers to increase throughput.

**Pricing:** $3,000–$5,000 per robot per month. Volume discounts for fleets (10+ robots). Minimum 12-month contract. Includes hardware, software, maintenance, support, deployment, and training.

**Industries:** E-commerce fulfillment, specialty retail distribution, pharmaceutical warehousing, omnichannel retail.

**Key Features:**
- **NaviLens AI** — machine learning optimizes picking routes in real-time
- [Fleet management](/guides/robot-fleet-management) and dynamic task allocation
- Advanced safety (cameras, LIDAR, speed limiting around humans)
- Integration with WMS systems (SAP, Oracle, custom)

**Best For:** Mid-to-large fulfillment operations (50,000+ sq ft) where labor costs and picking errors are significant.

**Pros:** Proven ROI — customers report 25–40% picking productivity gains. Easiest human-robot collaboration in warehouses. Transparent pricing.

**Cons:** $3k–$5k/month per robot is high for small operations. 12-month minimum. Requires WMS integration.

<Callout variant="info" title="Warehouse vs. manufacturing RaaS">
<p>RaaS companies split into two categories: <strong>manufacturing cobots</strong> (Formic, Rapid, Hirebotics) and <strong>warehouse AMRs</strong> (Locus, 6 River, Fetch). Manufacturing RaaS focuses on machine tending and welding. Warehouse RaaS focuses on picking, transport, and sortation. Make sure you are comparing apples to apples.</p>

### 6. 6 River Systems — Shopify-Backed Warehouse Fulfillment

Acquired by Shopify in 2019, 6 River Systems provides **Chuck**, an AMR specifically designed for warehouse fulfillment. Their RaaS model integrates directly with Shopify and other e-commerce platforms.

**Pricing:** Custom (typically $2,500–$4,500 per robot per month). Volume discounts for large deployments.

**Industries:** Shopify seller fulfillment, small parcel sort and pack operations, 3PL warehouses, e-commerce fulfillment centers.

**Key Features:**
- **Shopify integration** — direct API integration with Shopify orders
- Chuck AMR: fully autonomous picking and transport
- Designed specifically for small parcel fulfillment
- Mobile app for fleet oversight

**Best For:** Shopify sellers and 3PL operators wanting turnkey fulfillment automation.

**Pros:** Deep Shopify integration. Lower cost than Locus for parcel-scale operations. Strong company backing.

**Cons:** Built primarily for Shopify ecosystem. Less mature in non-Shopify environments.

**Industries:** Large distribution centers, e-commerce fulfillment, manufacturing logistics, 3PL warehouses.

**Key Features:**

- Zebra ecosystem integration: barcode scanning, RFID, mobile computing
- Cloud-based fleet management
- WMS integration (SAP, Oracle, custom)

**Best For:** Large distribution centers needing high-payload material handling, especially operations already using Zebra devices.

**Pros:** High payload and throughput. Seamless Zebra integration. Mature platform.

**Cons:** Less designed for human-robot picking collaboration. Zebra ecosystem lock-in.

**Industries:** Parcel sortation, retail returns processing, logistics final-mile hubs, beverage and CPG distribution.

**Best For:** Logistics hubs needing to automate picking of variable, deformable items. Strong for returns processing and parcel sortation.

**Pros:** Only solution for picking highly variable items. Computer vision improves over time. High picking speeds.

### 9. [Hirebotics](https://hirebotics.com/) — Turnkey Cobot Welding as a Service

Nashville-based Hirebotics rents complete [cobot welding](/guides/cobots-for-welding) cells (including robots, controllers, power supplies, training) for a flat monthly subscription. Zero capex, end-to-end responsibility.

**Pricing:** $2,000 per month for standard cell. Custom pricing for multi-robot installations. **Month-to-month contracts** (no long-term lock-in). Includes all hardware, maintenance, and technical support.

**Industries:** Metal fabrication job shops, contract manufacturers, heavy equipment builders, structural steel fabrication.

**Key Features:**
- **Complete cell delivery** — robot arm, controller, wire feeder, power supply all arrive pre-integrated
- Beamo software: proprietary weld quality feedback
- Remote troubleshooting and support
- Easy swap between MIG and TIG configurations

**Best For:** Smaller job shops and fabricators who want **instant welding automation without integration complexity**. Perfect for testing cobot welding before committing to purchase.

**Pros:** Lowest upfront cost for complete cobot welding. Flexible month-to-month terms. All-inclusive pricing.

**Cons:** $2,000/month = $24,000/year (comparable to a purchase over 2 years). Limited customization. Not ideal for high-utilization operations.

### 10. Covariant — Reinforcement Learning-Powered Picking

Berkeley-based Covariant trains AI models using reinforcement learning to autonomously perform robotic picking and packing tasks. Their RaaS offering deploys robotic arms with computer vision and learning-enabled software.

**Pricing:** Custom (typically $5,000–$15,000+/month per cell). Setup, training data collection, and ongoing model optimization included.

**Industries:** Retail picking and packing, e-commerce returns processing, cross-docking and parcel sortation, specialty logistics.

**Key Features:**
- **Reinforcement learning** — robots learn optimal picking strategies through trial, not explicit programming
- Vision system handles variable items, scales, orientations
- Works with various end effectors (vacuum, soft grippers, pinch)
- Fleet-wide learning: learnings shared across multiple robots

**Best For:** Advanced logistics operations with highly variable picking tasks where traditional automation has failed.

**Pros:** Most advanced AI approach to picking. Handles extreme item variability. Fleet-wide learning accelerates ROI.

**Cons:** Most expensive RaaS provider on this list. Technology still maturing. Requires significant training data during setup.

## How to Choose the Right RaaS Provider

### 1. Match Your Application Type

**Welding?** Formic or Hirebotics.
**Machine tending?** Rapid Robotics.
**Warehouse picking/transport?** Locus Robotics, 6 River Systems, Fetch Robotics.
**Variable-item picking?** Plus One Robotics, Covariant.
**Safety monitoring (existing robots)?** Veo Robotics.
**Multi-robot orchestration (large scale)?** Ready Robotics.

### 2. Compare 3-Year Total Cost

If you **cannot justify capex**, RaaS shifts the burden to monthly opex. But always calculate the **3-year total cost**:

- Buying a cobot system: $80,000 (capex) + $15,000 (maintenance/3 years) = $95,000
- RaaS for 3 years: $2,500/month x 36 months = $90,000

Use our [cobot ROI calculator](/guides/cobot-roi-calculator) to model your specific scenario. For lower-utilization equipment or seasonal demand, RaaS typically wins. For high-utilization (80%+ uptime), buying may be cheaper after year 2–3.

### 3. Consider Contract Flexibility

High-utilization, continuous operations? Buy (RaaS accumulates cost).
Seasonal or variable demand? RaaS (avoid idle capex).
Testing/validation? RaaS (minimize risk).
Month-to-month flexibility needed? Hirebotics (most flexible).
Okay with 12–24 month commitment? Locus, Fetch, 6 River (better pricing).

### 4. Assess Integration Complexity

Simple plug-and-play? Rapid Robotics, Hirebotics, Formic.
Requires WMS integration? Locus Robotics, Fetch Robotics.
Existing robot fleet control needed? Ready Robotics.
Standalone computer vision system? Plus One Robotics, Covariant.

### 5. Evaluate Vendor Lock-In Risk

Most RaaS providers use standard robots ([Universal Robots, Fanuc](/guides/best-cobot-arms)) you could buy if you leave. However:
- **Formic, Hirebotics, Rapid Robotics** use proprietary software (medium lock-in)
- **Locus, 6 River, Fetch** use proprietary fleet software (high lock-in)
- **Ready Robotics** explicitly supports multiple brands (low lock-in)

## When RaaS Makes Sense (And When It Doesn't)

For a deep dive on buy vs. rent economics, see our [Robotics as a Service guide](/guides/robotics-as-a-service).

**RaaS is a good fit** when you are testing automation before committing capital, have seasonal demand, operate with limited capital, face rapidly changing product mixes, or need to scale quickly without major capex.

**RaaS is not a good fit** for 24/7 high-utilization operations (buying is cheaper after 2–3 years), highly custom integrations, confidential processes, or geographically isolated facilities where support logistics are expensive.

## The RaaS Future: Key Trends

**1. AI-Powered Optimization.** Providers like Locus and Covariant are embedding machine learning into their platforms. Each deployment makes the next one better. Expect rapid improvements in picking speed, error rates, and efficiency.

**2. Horizontal Software Platforms.** Ready Robotics' multi-brand approach is winning. Expect more software-first players that abstract away robot hardware, letting customers mix-and-match equipment.

**3. Downward Price Pressure.** As competition increases, monthly subscription costs are trending down. Locus dropped prices 20% in 2024. Expect $2,000–$3,000/month to become the floor for warehouse AMRs.

**4. E-Commerce Platform Integration.** 6 River's Shopify integration is the template. Expect deep integrations with WMS, TMS, and order management systems. "Click to automate" will become the standard.

**5. Niche-Specific Solutions.** Broad-market providers will face competition from laser-focused vendors (Plus One for variable picking, Veo for safety). Specialization beats generalization.

<Callout variant="takeaway">
<p>The RaaS market is consolidating around two models: <strong>niche specialists</strong> (Formic for welding, Locus for warehouses) and <strong>horizontal platforms</strong> (Ready Robotics for multi-brand orchestration). Choose a specialist if your use case is clear, or a platform if you need flexibility across robot types.</p>
